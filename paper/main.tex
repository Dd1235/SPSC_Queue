\title{MPMC Queue Tradeoffs on an Apple M2 System:\\
A Reproducible Characterization}

\author{\IEEEauthorblockN{Dedeepya Avancha}
\IEEEauthorblockA{Independent Researcher\\
dedeepyaavancha@gmail.com}}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Influential concurrent-queue evaluations often use symmetric server systems,
thread affinity, and active cooling. We instead characterize four MPMC
designs---a bounded mutex queue, Michael--Scott with epoch-based reclamation,
Vyukov's bounded CAS-reservation queue, and an FAA ticket queue---plus an
industrial reference on one fanless Apple M2 client system. The matrix varies
producer:consumer ratio, oversubscription, capacity, and macOS QoS policy; QoS
is a scheduling policy, not observed core placement. Trials are process
isolated, interleaved by shape with cooldowns, and use schedule-to-dequeue latency to avoid
coordinated omission. At capacity 1024, $4\times$ oversubscription increased
FAA-ticket throughput by 77--94\% across three matrices while the CAS-reservation
queue lost about 80\%; the ticket arms also had roughly a $5\times$ lower
p99.9 than the next alternative. Both bounded algorithms lack a formal
lock-free progress guarantee, so this is a coordination-structure result, not
a progress-class comparison. Parent controls show that yielding backoff does
not rescue the CAS design and that FAA is not necessary for the ticket design's
qualitative profile; at capacity 64, however, both ticket variants collapse.
Requesting background rather than interactive QoS for all workers reverses the ranking,
although the experiment cannot attribute that change to a particular core
class. The mutex leads at the tested 4:4 and 2:6 $\times1$ shapes. Finally, the
Michael--Scott implementation reaches 742\,MB median peak RSS after a two-second
producer interval; replacing its unconditional limbo scan with prefix
reclamation cuts peak RSS by 76--82\% at consumer-heavy shapes and raises
end-to-end throughput by 28--76\%. The artifact provides raw CSVs, claim checks,
and scripted experiment profiles.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
``Lock-free'' is often used as performance shorthand: atomic operations should
beat a mutex, and a stronger progress guarantee should be more robust to
preemption. Neither inference follows from the definition. Client systems also
add heterogeneous cores, OS-controlled scheduling, and thermal limits that are
absent from many server-oriented evaluations.

This paper asks a narrow question: \emph{how do representative MPMC queue
tradeoffs change on one unpinnable, passively cooled Apple M2 system under
ratio, load, capacity, and scheduler-policy changes?} This is a case study, not
a general ranking or a claim of algorithmic novelty. Its contribution is the
characterization, the controls and boundaries around its explanations, two
methodology pitfalls, and a reproducible artifact.

Contributions:
\begin{itemize}
  \item A four-design comparison (bounded mutex, node-based Michael--Scott,
        bounded CAS reservation, and FAA ticket) in one harness, with
        ThreadSanitizer and invariant-checking stress tests. An SPSC queue is a
        specialization baseline; \texttt{moodycamel::ConcurrentQueue} is a
        contextual industrial reference with different ordering semantics.
  \item A client-silicon methodology: QoS-policy steering, oversubscription
        as a preemption proxy, coordinated-omission-aware paced latency,
        process-per-trial isolation, shape-wise interleaving with cooldowns and a
        QoS-steered calibration probe---plus two measured confounders (probe
        scheduling; launcher QoS inheritance) that silently distort na\"ive
        setups on macOS.
  \item Empirical boundaries rather than a universal winner: a
        capacity-dependent ticket/CAS contrast under oversubscription; ranking
        changes under QoS policy; mutex wins at two mixed-ratio shapes;
        per-design saturation knees under offered load
        (Sec.~\ref{sec:knees}); and an implementation-specific reclamation
        pathology analyzed with a successful prefix change, a failed heavier
        remediation, and an instrumented mechanism replication that separates
        maintenance cost from epoch advancement (Sec.~\ref{sec:ebrfix}).
\end{itemize}

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:background}
\textbf{Queue designs.} Michael and Scott's non-blocking queue
\cite{michael1996} anchors the node-based lineage, later refined by optimistic
\cite{ladanmozes2004} and wait-free \cite{kogan2011,yang2016} constructions;
safe memory reclamation --- hazard pointers \cite{michael2004}, epochs
\cite{fraser2004}, and their measured tradeoffs \cite{hart2007} --- is the
lineage's practical tax, and our results show it becoming the binding
constraint (Sec.~\ref{sec:mem}). Array-based designs begin with Tsigas and
Zhang \cite{tsigas2001}; Vyukov's bounded queue \cite{vyukov} popularized
per-cell sequence coordination. Its author explicitly does not claim formal
lock freedom: a reservation can be visible before its cell is published.
Fetch-and-add designs exploit FAA's
always-succeeds property, from the LMAX Disruptor's industrial sequencer
\cite{thompson2011} to LCRQ \cite{morrison2013}, SCQ \cite{nikolaev2019}, and
the wait-free bounded-memory wCQ \cite{nikolaev2022wcq}; BBQ
\cite{wang2022bbq} revisits bounded-queue design block-wise with explicit
attention to deployment regimes; flat combining \cite{hendler2010} reframes
the contention question entirely. \texttt{moodycamel::ConcurrentQueue}
\cite{moodycamel} and \texttt{rigtorp::MPMCQueue} \cite{rigtorp} represent
deployed industrial practice.

\textbf{Preemption and synchronization.} The interaction of multiprogramming
with non-blocking algorithms is a 1990s question: scheduler-conscious
synchronization \cite{kontothanassis1997} and Michael and Scott's
preemption-safe locking study \cite{michael1998preemption} found non-blocking
algorithms \emph{more} robust than locks under multiprogramming on the SMPs
of that era. Our H1 was essentially their conclusion restated as a
prediction; Sec.~\ref{sec:oversub} shows that on this platform the outcome
splits along coordination structure rather than blocking versus non-blocking.

\textbf{Characterization studies.} Our genre follows David et al.'s
cross-platform synchronization study \cite{david2013}, which showed
synchronization conclusions to be strongly hardware-dependent, and Guiroux et
al.\ \cite{guiroux2016}, who showed lock rankings shift with workload and
machine. Relative to both: they varied server hardware under pinned threads;
we hold one \emph{client} system fixed and vary the scheduling regime
(placement policy, preemption pressure, offered load) that client software
cannot control. The two axes are complementary, and our single-system scope
is the price of the second axis.

\textbf{Asymmetric multicores.} Single-ISA heterogeneous processors were
studied for energy scheduling well before they reached the desktop
\cite{fedorova2009}; ARM's big.LITTLE \cite{greenhalgh2011} and Intel's hybrid
Alder Lake \cite{rotem2022} made them mainstream. That literature concerns
\emph{scheduler} policy. Our case study measures responses to macOS QoS
policies, but without core-residency traces it cannot isolate heterogeneity
from other priority and scheduling effects.

\textbf{Methodology.} We follow Hoefler and Belli's reporting guidance
\cite{hoefler2015}, Tene's coordinated-omission critique \cite{tene}, and take
Mytkowicz et al.'s warning about invisible experimental bias
\cite{mytkowicz2009} as standing orders --- Sec.~\ref{sec:method} documents
two such biases we caught in our own instruments.

\textbf{Scope relative to prior work.} The evaluated literature leaves room
for an artifact-backed MPMC case study on an ARM client system with
OS-controlled placement. We do not claim an exhaustive literature review.

Four implementation notes matter for interpretation. \emph{Michael--Scott}
copies the value before its dequeue CAS (per the original) and retires nodes
through a 3-epoch reclamation scheme whose advancement requires every
in-operation thread to have observed the current epoch---a property that
Sec.~\ref{sec:mem} shows becoming costly. Although the Michael--Scott pointer
protocol is lock-free, allocation, vector-backed EBR maintenance, and an orphan
list mutex mean this complete implementation is not a pure lock-free operation.
\emph{Vyukov} reserves cursor positions by CAS with unbounded retry; a thread
preempted after reservation but before sequence publication can delay a FIFO
predecessor, so the protocol is mutex-free but not formally lock-free
\cite{vyukov}. The \emph{FAA ticket} queue reserves with one fetch-and-add (no
claim losers), but its irrevocable ticket similarly makes completion
blocking-on-cell and forbids a try-API. The harness therefore terminates
consumers with poison pills rather than a racy shared counter. We build two
within-parent controls:
\textsf{vyukov-b} (Vyukov plus exactly the ticket queue's 1024-spin-then-yield
politeness in every retry path) and \textsf{casticket} (the ticket queue with
its fetch-and-add claim replaced by a raw CAS-increment loop). Each control
differs from its own parent in one property only; the cross-family comparison
still differs in reservation, cell protocol, and API semantics. Finally,
\texttt{moodycamel::ConcurrentQueue} with implicit producers is not globally
linearizable across producers, and its constructor argument is a preallocation
estimate rather than a strict bound. We therefore use it as industrial context,
not a semantics- or capacity-matched arm.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}
\textbf{Platform.} The reported collection platform was a MacBook Air
(Mac14,2), Apple M2 (4 P- + 4 E-cores), 8\,GB RAM, passive cooling, macOS
15.6.1 (24G90), Apple clang 17.0.0, \texttt{-O3}. The historical rows encode
configuration and time, but not OS/build; the exact OS fields were recorded
when the same machine was inspected during artifact finalization.

\textbf{Harness and metric.} One fresh process measures one configuration and
appends one self-describing CSV row. The payload is a \texttt{uint64\_t}; the
default strict capacity is 1024 for the custom bounded queues and mutex (MS
ignores it, and moodycamel treats it only as a preallocation estimate). In
saturated mode, the harness launches producers, sleeps for two seconds, signals
them to stop, drains all accepted messages, sends one poison pill per consumer,
and joins the consumers. Reported Mops/s is completed real dequeues divided by this full
makespan---one completed message, not enqueue plus dequeue, counts as one. The
metric consequently includes startup, drain, and worker/TLS teardown, which is
important for interpreting MS reclamation.

\textbf{Matrix and statistics.} The driver keeps different queues for a shape
close in time, with 0.3\,s between processes and 2\,s between rounds, but uses a
fixed queue order rather than randomization. A dependent-arithmetic calibration
loop precedes every trial. Trial~0 is warmup; points are medians and error bars
are IQRs over measured trials \cite{hoefler2015}. Headline v2 planned 92
queue/configuration arms and analyzes 91 after the documented incompatible arm
is excluded. It uses rounds 0--7 (one warmup plus seven measured); an
interrupted round~8 remains in the raw file but is excluded. The v3 control
and h3 reclamation matrices likewise use one warmup plus seven measured rounds,
apart from explicitly reported timeouts. The earlier v1 matrix (one warmup plus
four measured rounds) is a replication check. These are descriptive statistics;
we do not report inferential significance tests, but headline effect medians
carry 95\% bootstrap percentile intervals (20{,}000 resamples, fixed seed;
\texttt{scripts/bootstrap\_ci.py}). At seven kept rounds these intervals are
coarse by construction; we quote them so the reader can see that coarseness.

\textbf{Latency.} Producers are staggered and paced at a total offered load of
1\,M message/s. Payloads carry the \emph{scheduled} send time, so
schedule-to-dequeue latency charges producer lateness instead of omitting it
\cite{tene}. The historical v3 executable used fixed per-consumer sample
buffers. They retained every sample for the ticket and mutex arms, but dropped
at most 2.17\% in one of seven MS, Vyukov, and Vyukov-backoff trials and two of
seven moodycamel trials at $4\times$. Because those buffers retained the
earliest samples, truncation can bias tails downward; the analysis pipeline
therefore excludes incomplete rows from plotted and quoted latency medians.
Current HEAD instead uses growable vectors and aborts if the expected, popped,
and retained counts disagree, but that correction does not alter the committed
v3 measurements.

\textbf{QoS and oversubscription.} macOS exposes no usable affinity API on this
system. The historical harness requested \texttt{USER\_INTERACTIVE} or
\texttt{BACKGROUND} for workers, but did not record the call result, actual core
residency, or frequency; results are attributed to the requested QoS policy,
not to P- or E-cores. Current HEAD aborts a new trial if a QoS request fails.
Multiplying both thread counts at a fixed ratio creates runnable-thread pressure and serves
as a preemption proxy. Process-level involuntary context switches from
\texttt{getrusage} corroborate that pressure but are not scheduler traces.

\textbf{Two measured confounders.} (i)~With default QoS, the calibration probe
spanned ${\sim}1.77\times$; the later probe requesting interactive QoS had a
narrower range, showing that the original ``thermal'' instrument was also
sensitive to scheduling policy.
The residual ${\sim}1.6\times$ can mix DVFS, temperature, and remaining
placement variation. (ii)~Workers inherit the launcher's QoS. A background
launcher in v1 was associated with $10$--$30\times$ larger $\times1$ p99.9.
Before creating workers, v2 requested \texttt{QOS\_CLASS\_DEFAULT} for the
spawning thread; no absolute latency in the paper comes from v1.

\textbf{Diagnostic build and thermal screen.} Retry, cursor-re-read, and
slot-yield counters use shared atomic increments in a separate stats binary
\cite{mytkowicz2009}. The canonical v3s profile is only a 1.5\,s run with one
warmup plus two measured rounds; its magnitudes are diagnostic and may be
perturbed by instrumentation. Headline performance is uninstrumented. In v2,
per-queue calibration medians differ by about 1.6\%, and throughput shows no
monotone round-wise decline larger than the reported variation. This screen
reduces concern about drift but cannot remove fixed-order bias.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Throughput across ratios: the 1:1 mirage}
\begin{figure}[t]\centering
\maybefig{fig_throughput_ratio}{\linewidth}
\caption{Median completed-message throughput by P:C ratio, capacity 1024,
default QoS. Error bars: IQR over seven measured trials.}
\label{fig:ratio}
\end{figure}
At 1:1 the array queues lead (FAA 127.7, Vyukov 118.4 vs mutex 34.7 Mops/s).
One step to 2:2 erases ${\sim}79\%$ of both (FAA 26.3, Vyukov 25.2), while the
mutex loses one third (23.3) but does not yet lead. At 4:4 the mutex leads every
MPMC arm (19.0 vs FAA 16.5, Vyukov and moodycamel 7.8, MS 6.2); it also narrowly
   leads at 2:6 (15.9 vs FAA 14.6). The SPSC baseline (373.6 at 1:1) is
${\sim}3\times$ the best MPMC result in the only shape where that specialization
is legal. Thus neither the 1:1 ranking nor the mutex win is universal even
within this matrix.

\subsection{Oversubscription: CAS reservation versus ticket/turn}
\label{sec:oversub}

Our a priori claims ledger predicted that an irrevocable FAA reservation would
be especially vulnerable when a claimant is descheduled. Subsequent taxonomy
review matters: Vyukov also reserves before publishing and is not formally
lock-free, so the experiment does not invert two formal progress classes. It
instead contrasts two reservation structures. Across three independently run
matrices, capacity-1024 FAA throughput rises 77--94\% from $\times1$ to
$\times4$ (v3: 16.5$\,\to\,$29.7 Mops/s; $\times4/\times1$ ratio CI95
[1.75, 1.88]), while Vyukov falls about 80\% (7.9$\,\to\,$1.7; ratio CI95
[0.19, 0.23]); the mutex falls 24\% and MS 15\%. The two intervals sit on
opposite sides of 1 with a wide gap. In complete-sample
latency trials, CAS/ticket and FAA/ticket have 14.1 and 14.2\,ms p99.9. The next
best arm is the mutex at 69.5\,ms; Vyukov, Vyukov-backoff, MS, and moodycamel
range from 70.6 to 107.5\,ms. The ticket result is therefore about $5\times$
lower than the nearest alternative, without relying on truncated trials.

\textbf{Evidence from parent controls (Table~\ref{tab:controls}).}
\textsf{vyukov-b} changes only Vyukov's retry politeness. It follows the same
throughput collapse (8.0$\,\to\,$1.7 Mops/s), and its complete-trial p99.9 is
75.0\,ms versus 70.6\,ms, so this yielding policy does not rescue the design.
\textsf{casticket} changes only the FAA ticket parent's claim instruction. It
retains a rising profile (7.1$\,\to\,$19.3) and a 14.1\,ms tail, showing that
FAA is not necessary for the qualitative ticket result; FAA is nevertheless
1.5--2.3$\times$ faster than CAS/ticket at the two endpoints. Because no
single-variable control transforms Vyukov's cell protocol into the ticket/turn
protocol, these comparisons are \emph{consistent with}, but do not isolate,
the broader ticket/turn structure as the source of robustness.

The finding is capacity-conditional (Table~\ref{tab:capacity}). At capacity 64
both ticket designs fall to 2.6 Mops/s, consistent with a descheduled ticket
holder exhausting the available slack. Capacity 1024 gives the strongest
ticket result among the three values tested; 8192 is slower than 1024 for both
ticket arms. A larger cache footprint is one plausible explanation for that
non-monotonicity, but we did not measure cache misses. Three capacities establish
a boundary in this experiment, not an if-and-only-if law.

\subsection{Diagnostic mechanism counters}
\begin{figure}[t]\centering
\maybefig[v3s]{fig_mechanism}{\linewidth}
\caption{Diagnostic 1.5\,s stats build (one warmup, two measured rounds):
retry/claim failures per completed message (left) and process involuntary
context switches (right), log scales.}
\label{fig:mech}
\end{figure}
At $\times4$, the canonical stats run records 0.752 failed cursor CASes and
1.687 behind-cursor re-reads per completed message for Vyukov. Those events
touch its shared cursors. FAA has exactly zero claim failures by construction;
its 0.057 recorded slot-yield events per message occur only after 1024 turn
loads, so event counts across protocols are not unit-cost comparable. The
process-level median involuntary-switch counts are 84.9k for Vyukov, 57.0k for
Vyukov-backoff, and 919.9k for FAA. Both CAS variants collapse despite different
counts, while the fastest arm has far more, so raw switch count does not explain
the ordering. Together these diagnostics support a shared-cursor-contention
explanation, but two measured rounds, perturbing atomic counters, and no cache
coherence measurements preclude a causal claim.

\subsection{QoS policy reorders the ranking}
\begin{figure}[t]\centering
\maybefig{fig_qos}{\linewidth}
\caption{Throughput at 4P:4C, capacity 1024, under requested macOS QoS
policies; historical call success and actual core residency were not recorded.}
\label{fig:qos}
\end{figure}
In canonical v2, requesting background rather than interactive QoS for all workers
retains 88\% of FAA throughput (16.7$\,\to\,$14.7 Mops/s; CI95 [83, 95]\%),
19\% of the mutex (19.1$\,\to\,$3.6; [15, 20]\%), and 6\% of Vyukov
(8.1$\,\to\,$0.46; [5, 6]\%). Thus background QoS
reverses the interactive-policy mutex/FAA order; FAA is about $4\times$ the
mutex and $32\times$ Vyukov under the background policy. These effects are
associated with the requested scheduler policies, not observations of the
effective class. They may involve core-class choice, frequency, priority, and timeslicing,
none of which the harness observes. Role-specific policies also
differ by design: MS is lower with producers background than with consumers
background (2.1 vs 4.3 Mops/s), while moodycamel shows the opposite direction
(4.5 vs 2.1). This is consistent with side-specific work, but does not identify
allocation or recycling as the cause.

One of seven all-background rounds was a severe outlier: FAA completed no real
messages, while Vyukov, mutex, and moodycamel fell to 0.092, 0.174, and
0.253\,Mops/s; MS remained at 2.65. FAA led the other six paired rounds. The
median/IQR summary therefore describes the central policy response, not
round-level reliability under background scheduling.

\subsection{Process memory as an unbounded-implementation failure mode}
\label{sec:mem}
The MS queue never reports full. Over the full trial (two-second producer
interval plus drain and teardown), median process peak RSS is 376\,MB at 1:1
and \textbf{742\,MB at 1:7}, versus 1--2\,MB for each strictly bounded custom
design. At 7:1, MS uses only 7\,MB. This consumer-count asymmetry implicates the
dequeue-side EBR path: each dequeue retires a node, every 64 retirements attempts
epoch advancement, and the legacy path scans the thread's entire limbo vector.
It does not, however, partition RSS into live queue nodes, retired nodes, vector
capacity, or transient orphan-list handoff at thread exit. We therefore treat
peak RSS as an end-to-end implementation metric rather than a pure queue-footprint
measurement.

\subsection{Remediating the reclamation pathology}
\label{sec:ebrfix}
\begin{figure}[t]\centering
\maybefig[h3]{fig_ebrfix}{\linewidth}
\caption{EBR remediation modes (one warmup, seven measured rounds): full-trial
peak RSS (log) and end-to-end throughput for legacy scanning, prefix
reclamation, a failed heavier retry bundle, and the bounded mutex reference.}
\label{fig:ebrfix}
\end{figure}
The three modes are runtime-selectable in the same build. Legacy reclamation
always scans and compacts the full limbo vector, $O(\text{limbo})$, even when
nothing is eligible. Retire epochs are nondecreasing, so the prefix mode stops
at the first survivor. Its vector erase still shifts survivors: the general
cost is expired entries visited plus survivors shifted, not merely the number
freed. Its decisive code-path difference is the stuck-epoch case, where no
entry expires and it returns in $O(1)$ instead of scanning all limbo entries.

At 1:7 and 2:6, prefix mode reduces median peak RSS by 76--82\%
($626\!\to\!153$ and $536\!\to\!97$\,MB; 1:7 legacy/fix ratio CI95
[2.6, 4.6]$\times$) and raises end-to-end throughput by
76\% and 28\% (1:7: $5.4\!\to\!9.4$\,Mops/s; ratio CI95 [1.55, 1.86]). At 4:4, prefix mode cuts RSS
52\% but reduces throughput 13\%. Absolute legacy RSS differs between v2 and
h3 (742 vs 626\,MB at 1:7), so every percentage above is a within-h3
comparison.

\begin{figure}[t]\centering
\maybefig[h3s]{fig_ebrmech}{\linewidth}
\caption{Instrumented reclamation mechanism (stats build, h3s): median
maintenance-pass duration, epoch-advance success rate, and peak per-thread
limbo size, per mode and ratio.}
\label{fig:ebrmech}
\end{figure}
\textbf{Measuring the mechanism.} An instrumented replication (matrix h3s,
same shapes, stats build) records each link of the hypothesized loop
separately: maintenance-pass duration (the cost link), epoch-advance success
rate (the stuck-epoch link), and peak per-thread limbo size (the backlog
link); Fig.~\ref{fig:ebrmech}. The RSS and throughput effects replicate under
instrumentation (1:7 legacy${\to}$prefix: $578\!\to\!227$\,MB,
$5.4\!\to\!10.4$\,Mops/s). Three observations discipline the explanation.
First, at 1:7 the prefix mode cuts median maintenance cost from 79.6 to
21.3\,\textmu s per pass while the advance success rate stays below 0.3\% in
\emph{both} modes: the fix works without unsticking the epoch, by making
stuck-epoch passes cheap. Its peak limbo is accordingly \emph{larger}
(929k vs.\ 129k entries) --- the backlog spikes, then clears in bulk --- while
total memory is 2.6$\times$ lower. Second, the retry bundle achieves exactly
what it was designed for --- its advance success rate reaches 69\% at 1:7 and
its limbo peak is the smallest of the three --- and still posts the worst
memory (645\,MB) and throughput (2.6\,Mops/s), with 168\,\textmu s passes.
Epoch advancement is therefore neither necessary (prefix wins with the epoch
still stuck) nor sufficient (retry unsticks it and loses); the binding
quantity is maintenance cost in the consumers' pop path. Third, at 1:1 both
legacy and prefix show a healthy reclamation system --- ${\ge}99.8\%$ advance
success, ${\sim}4.6$\,\textmu s passes, peak limbo ${\le}3.5$k entries ---
yet both retain ${\sim}550$--$600$\,MB. The counters exonerate reclamation
lag at 1:1; the remaining candidate is live-queue growth (an unthrottled
producer outrunning a consumer that pays retire-and-maintain per pop), which
we did not instrument directly. A bounded queue would exert back-pressure
here; the unbounded design converts the rate mismatch into memory.

The \textsf{ms-retry} arm remains a heavier \emph{bundle}: prefix scanning,
maintenance deferred until unpinned, up to 64 advancement probes under
pressure, and an amortized registry scan every 256 pins. The counters now
apportion part of the blame --- the probes succeed, so the loss is not failed
advancement --- but the residual split between probe cost and registry-scan
coherence traffic stays unmeasured. Hart et al.~\cite{hart2007} showed that
reclamation scheme choice affects performance; here, implementation details
within one EBR scheme are themselves material.

\subsection{Finite-window work balance}
\begin{figure}[t]\centering
\maybefig[v3]{fig_fairness}{\linewidth}
\caption{CoV of per-thread completed counts at 4P:4C, capacity 1024. Lower is
more balanced over the finite trial; this is not a liveness guarantee.}
\label{fig:fair}
\end{figure}
The coefficient of variation (CoV) of per-thread counts measures finite-window
work balance, not starvation freedom (Fig.~\ref{fig:fair}). At capacity 1024,
FAA's median producer CoV rises from 0.0017 at $\times1$ to only 0.0076 at
$\times4$; CAS/ticket is 0.014 and 0.010. Ticket order is FIFO among issued
claims, but a frequently scheduled thread can issue more claims, so the low CoV
is an empirical workload result rather than fairness by construction. At
$\times4$, median producer CoV for the other arms ranges from 0.073 (mutex) to
0.150 (MS). Moodycamel's producer-heavy v2 medians reach 0.18--0.19, consistent
with its per-producer subqueues and weaker global ordering. Sequential thread
launch can also contribute startup skew; longer-run fairness deserves separate
study.

\subsection{Non-oversubscribed latency, and an incompatibility}
Using v3 consistently, $\times1$ 4:4 p50 is below 1.8\,$\mu$s for every arm
(FAA 375\,ns; mutex 1.79\,$\mu$s). At p99, mutex reaches 262\,$\mu$s and MS
21.2\,$\mu$s; the others range from 1.8 to 12.5\,$\mu$s. Median p99.9 is
24\,$\mu$s for Vyukov, 45\,$\mu$s for Vyukov-backoff, 0.206\,ms for
CAS/ticket, 0.583\,ms for FAA, and 0.737\,ms for moodycamel, versus 8.20\,ms
for MS and 6.10\,ms for the mutex. These medians show substantial run-to-run
tail variability; they do not establish a stable $\times1$ ordering.

Separately, with the constructor capacity argument set to 64 at $\times4$,
moodycamel completes only the v2 warmup row and no measured round before the
120\,s timeout, during drain/poison handling. Because 64 is only a preallocation
estimate for this library, we report a harness/configuration incompatibility,
not a strict-capacity result or a throughput score.

\subsection{Saturation knees under offered load}
\label{sec:knees}
\begin{figure}[t]\centering
\maybefig[loadfull]{fig_loadcurve}{0.9\linewidth}
\caption{p99 schedule-to-dequeue latency vs.\ offered load at 4:4 $\times1$,
capacity 1024 (log--log; load + load2 sweeps). Post-knee values measure
accumulated backlog, by construction of the coordinated-omission-aware
timestamping.}
\label{fig:loadcurve}
\end{figure}
Sweeping the offered rate from 0.25 to 24\,M msg/s at the 4:4 dedicated shape
locates each design's saturation knee (Fig.~\ref{fig:loadcurve}). Because
payloads carry scheduled send times, once offered load exceeds sustainable
throughput the reported latency grows toward seconds of accumulated backlog
--- the curve's vertical segment is the knee, not a tail measurement. Below
saturation (through 4\,M msg/s) every design delivers sub-millisecond p99 and
the differences are scheduler-noise dominated. The knees order as the
saturated-throughput ranking predicts: Michael--Scott and the mutex saturate
between 4 and 6\,M msg/s, Vyukov between 6 and 8, and both ticket variants
last --- the FAA queue still posts a 0.4\,\textmu s median at 8\,M msg/s,
where the CAS/ticket variant has climbed to 25\,\textmu s and every other
design is at millisecond scale or beyond. The mutex is the only design with a
visible pre-knee gradient (median 0.8${\to}$52\,\textmu s across
0.25${\to}$4\,M), degrading gracefully where the reservation-based designs
run flat and then cliff. moodycamel rows at ${\ge}8$\,M msg/s are excluded:
past its knee, the poison-pill drain protocol's exact-accounting invariant
fails probabilistically (a pill can overtake older data across its
sub-queues, stranding messages), which the harness reports as an abort rather
than a biased tail; its exact-accounting curve ends at 6\,M msg/s,
unsaturated.

\section{Threats to Validity}
\label{sec:threats}
\textbf{External and construct validity.} This is one 8\,GB M2 Air, OS, and
compiler; it establishes no client-wide or cross-architecture ranking. QoS
class is not core placement, and oversubscription is only a preemption proxy.
We lack scheduler traces, residency, frequency, power, temperature, and cache-
coherence counters. Accordingly, explanations based on scheduling or shared-
cursor traffic are hypotheses consistent with controls and diagnostics.

\textbf{Measurement validity.} Queue order is fixed, workers have no start
barrier, and the throughput timer starts before producers are launched
sequentially. Two-second medians reduce but do not eliminate order and startup
skew. The throughput denominator includes drain and EBR teardown; process peak
RSS includes live data, retired objects, allocator/vector capacity, and possible
transient orphan handoff. Historical v3 fixed latency buffers truncate up to
2.17\% of samples in the disclosed non-ticket trials, so the pipeline excludes
those rows; the current exact-accounting path corrects this for new runs but
does not retroactively repair them. Seven measured rounds support descriptive
medians and IQRs, not small-effect or multiple-comparison inference; mechanism
counters have only two measured rounds and can perturb the retry paths they
count.

\textbf{Implementation validity.} The custom queues are study implementations,
not each design's best-known optimized version. The MS findings include this
specific allocator and EBR, not the Michael--Scott algorithm or EBR generally.
The two controls are single-change only relative to their own parents; there is
no single-change bridge between the CAS ring and ticket/turn protocol.
Moodycamel differs in global ordering and capacity semantics, so it is context,
not a matched control. Stress invariants and ThreadSanitizer catch many safety
failures but do not prove linearizability or progress. Replication on other
systems, randomized trial order, synchronized starts, fresh measurements with
the corrected latency path, and direct scheduler/performance counters remain
necessary.

\section{Conclusion}
On this M2 system, no design wins every tested regime. The 1:1 array-queue lead
does not survive all mixed ratios; the mutex leads at 4:4 and 2:6; the
capacity-1024 ticket protocol improves under oversubscription while the CAS
reservation ring collapses; and changing QoS policy reverses their ranking
without revealing which cores ran them. Parent controls rule out one yielding
policy as a remedy and show that FAA is not required for the ticket profile,
but do not by themselves prove the ticket/turn protocol is causal. Capacity 64
restores the predicted reservation stall.

For this MS+EBR implementation, process memory is as important as speed. Prefix
reclamation materially improves consumer-heavy shapes, has a throughput cost at
4:4, and leaves 1:1 unresolved; the heavier remediation bundle fails. The
practical lesson is conditional: measure completed-message makespan, tails,
memory, work balance, capacity, and scheduler policy in the intended deployment
rather than extrapolating from 1:1 throughput or from labels such as
``lock-free.''
