\title{MPMC Queue Tradeoffs on Asymmetric Client Silicon:\\
A Characterization Study on Apple M2}

\author{\IEEEauthorblockN{Dedeepya Avancha}
\IEEEauthorblockA{Independent / [affiliation]\\
dedeepyaavancha@gmail.com}}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Published evaluations of concurrent queues overwhelmingly assume symmetric x86
server cores, pinned threads, and active cooling. Client devices violate all
three. We characterize four classic multi-producer/multi-consumer designs---a
mutex-guarded queue, the Michael--Scott lock-free queue with epoch-based
reclamation, Vyukov's bounded CAS queue, and a fetch-and-add (FAA) ticket
queue---plus an industrial reference, on an Apple M2 (4 performance + 4
efficiency cores), across producer:consumer ratios, QoS-steered core placement,
and oversubscription, using coordinated-omission-aware latency measurement and
thermally interleaved trials. Three results contradict the textbook ranking.
(1)~Our pre-registered hypothesis---that the FAA ticket queue's
blocking-on-slot completion would collapse under preemption---\emph{inverted}:
under $4\times$ oversubscription the FAA queue improved by 77--94\% while the
lock-free CAS queue lost ${\sim}80\%$, with a ${\sim}5\times$ tail advantage.
Two single-variable controls decompose the inversion: a yielding-backoff
variant of the CAS queue changes nothing (spin policy eliminated), while a
CAS-claim variant of the ticket queue retains the robustness (the ticket/turn
slot discipline, not the atomic primitive, prevents the collapse) at half the
throughput (the FAA primitive's measured contribution). Instrumented builds
confirm the mechanism: the CAS queue pays 0.7 failed CASes plus 1.8
shared-cursor re-reads per operation, where ticket designs wait on private
cells. A capacity control bounds the finding: with only 64 slots of slack,
blocking-on-slot re-emerges and the textbook prediction returns. (2)~Efficiency-core
placement reorders the ranking entirely: the FAA queue retains 96\% of its
throughput on E-cores while the mutex retains 13\% and the CAS queue 6\%.
(3)~At moderate mixed ratios on dedicated cores, the mutex baseline beats every
lock-free design. We additionally measure the unbounded queue's true failure
mode (742\,MB resident growth in a two-second run, traced to epoch-reclamation
starvation under consumer parallelism) and near-perfect producer fairness for
FAA ticketing. All results reproduce with one command from our open artifact.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Lock-free queues are folklore-fast: compare-and-swap beats locks, stronger
progress guarantees cost complexity but buy robustness. That folklore was
established on server-class symmetric multiprocessors with pinned threads.
Most software instead runs on client silicon: heterogeneous cores, opaque OS
scheduling, passive cooling.

This paper asks a narrow, answerable question: \emph{how much of the textbook
MPMC-queue tradeoff story survives contact with an asymmetric, unpinnable,
thermally limited client SoC?} We claim no algorithmic novelty; the
contribution is a careful characterization, two methodology pitfalls we
document for others, and a reproducible artifact.

Contributions:
\begin{itemize}
  \item A four-design comparison (blocking, lock-free node-based, lock-free
        array CAS, FAA ticket) in one harness, validated with ThreadSanitizer
        and invariant-checking stress tests, with
        \texttt{moodycamel::ConcurrentQueue} as an industrial reference and an
        SPSC queue as the specialization baseline.
  \item A client-silicon methodology: QoS-class core steering, oversubscription
        as a preemption proxy, coordinated-omission-aware paced latency,
        process-per-trial isolation, thermal round-robin interleaving with a
        pinned calibration probe---plus two measured confounders (probe
        placement; launcher QoS inheritance) that silently distort na\"ive
        setups on macOS.
  \item Findings F1--F5 (Sec.~\ref{sec:results}), including a controlled
        decomposition of the central surprise: preemption robustness is a
        property of the ticket/turn \emph{slot discipline}, not of the claim
        primitive, spin policy, or progress-guarantee class --- established
        with two single-variable control variants and per-operation retry
        counters, and bounded by a capacity control. Plus: the E-core
        reordering; the mutex winning the dedicated-core middle; memory (not
        speed) as the unbounded design's failure mode, traced to
        epoch-advancement starvation; and fairness as a free consequence of
        ticketing.
\end{itemize}

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:background}
\textbf{Queue designs.} Michael and Scott's non-blocking queue
\cite{michael1996} anchors the node-based lineage, later refined by optimistic
\cite{ladanmozes2004} and wait-free \cite{kogan2011,yang2016} constructions;
safe memory reclamation --- hazard pointers \cite{michael2004}, epochs
\cite{fraser2004}, and their measured tradeoffs \cite{hart2007} --- is the
lineage's practical tax, and our results show it becoming the binding
constraint (Sec.~\ref{sec:mem}). Array-based designs begin with Tsigas and
Zhang \cite{tsigas2001}; Vyukov's bounded queue \cite{vyukov} popularized
per-cell sequence coordination. Fetch-and-add designs exploit FAA's
always-succeeds property, from the LMAX Disruptor's industrial sequencer
\cite{thompson2011} to LCRQ \cite{morrison2013} and SCQ \cite{nikolaev2019};
flat combining \cite{hendler2010} reframes the contention question entirely.
\texttt{moodycamel::ConcurrentQueue} \cite{moodycamel} represents deployed
industrial practice.

\textbf{Characterization studies.} Our genre follows David et al.'s
cross-platform synchronization study \cite{david2013}, which showed
synchronization conclusions to be strongly hardware-dependent, and Guiroux et
al.\ \cite{guiroux2016}, who showed lock rankings shift with workload and
machine --- both on symmetric servers. We extend the genre's central lesson
to asymmetric client silicon.

\textbf{Asymmetric multicores.} Single-ISA heterogeneous processors were
studied for energy scheduling well before they reached the desktop
\cite{fedorova2009}; ARM's big.LITTLE \cite{greenhalgh2011} and Intel's hybrid
Alder Lake \cite{rotem2022} made them mainstream. That literature concerns
\emph{scheduler} policy; how heterogeneity reshuffles \emph{lock-free
algorithm} tradeoffs has received little direct measurement.

\textbf{Methodology.} We follow Hoefler and Belli's reporting guidance
\cite{hoefler2015}, Tene's coordinated-omission critique \cite{tene}, and take
Mytkowicz et al.'s warning about invisible experimental bias
\cite{mytkowicz2009} as standing orders --- Sec.~\ref{sec:method} documents
two such biases we caught in our own instruments.

\textbf{Gap.} We are not aware of a systematic MPMC queue characterization on
asymmetric ARM client silicon under OS-controlled placement.

Three implementation notes matter for interpretation. \emph{Michael--Scott}
copies the value before its dequeue CAS (per the original) and retires nodes
through a 3-epoch reclamation scheme whose advancement requires every
in-operation thread to have observed the current epoch---a property that
Sec.~\ref{sec:mem} shows becoming the bottleneck. \emph{Vyukov} claims cursor
positions by CAS with unbounded individual retry. The \emph{FAA ticket} queue's
claim is a single fetch-and-add (wait-free, no losers); its tickets are
irrevocable, which makes completion blocking-on-slot and forbids a try-API ---
our harness therefore terminates consumers with poison pills rather than
shared counters. To decompose causes rather than compare bundles of
differences, we additionally build two \emph{single-variable controls}:
\textsf{vyukov-b} (Vyukov plus exactly the ticket queue's 1024-spin-then-yield
politeness in every retry path) and \textsf{casticket} (the ticket queue with
its fetch-and-add claim replaced by a raw CAS-increment loop). Each control
differs from its parent in one property only.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}
\textbf{Platform.} Apple M2 (MacBook Air, passively cooled), 4 P- + 4 E-cores,
macOS; Apple clang 17, \texttt{-O3}.

\textbf{Harness.} One process per trial appends one CSV row; the matrix driver
interleaves queues round-robin \emph{within} each trial round, so cross-queue
comparisons occur under near-identical thermal state. A fixed
dependent-arithmetic calibration loop is timed before every trial. Trial~0 per
configuration is warmup; we report medians with IQR over the remaining trials
\cite{hoefler2015}. The canonical dataset (v2) contains 8 complete rounds of
92 configurations; a prior full run (v1) serves as a replication check ---
headline throughput medians agree within ${\sim}6\%$. Across configurations
the median coefficient of variation is 2.0\% (p90: 6.4\%).

\textbf{Latency.} Producers are paced against a fixed schedule (offered load
split across producers, staggered); payloads carry the \emph{scheduled} send
time, so producer lateness is charged to the reported tail rather than
omitted \cite{tene}.

\textbf{Placement and preemption.} macOS exposes no thread affinity on Apple
silicon; we steer placement with thread QoS
(\texttt{USER\_INTERACTIVE}$\,\to\,$P-cores,
\texttt{BACKGROUND}$\,\to\,$E-cores) and treat it as a bias, not an
assignment. Oversubscription (both thread counts multiplied at fixed ratio)
serves as a controlled preemption generator.

\textbf{Two confounders we measured so others can avoid them.}
(i)~\emph{Probe placement:} with default QoS our calibration loop sometimes
landed on an E-core; its spread (${\sim}1.77\times$) tracked the P/E speed
ratio, not temperature. Pinning the probe to interactive QoS narrowed the
spread; the residual (${\sim}1.6\times$ worst case) is genuine
frequency/thermal variation. (ii)~\emph{Launcher QoS inheritance:} macOS
worker threads inherit the spawning thread's QoS. Launched from a background
shell, all v1 workers ran silently demoted, inflating dedicated-core p99.9 by
$10$--$30\times$. Explicitly resetting worker QoS
(\texttt{QOS\_CLASS\_DEFAULT}) fixed this in v2; v1's \emph{cross-queue}
comparisons survived only because interleaving demoted all arms equally.
Absolute latencies in this paper cite v2 exclusively.

\textbf{Instrumented builds.} Mechanism counters (per-operation CAS
failures, cursor re-reads, slot-wait yields) are compiled only into a separate
\emph{stats} binary used for the mechanism dataset (v3s); headline numbers come
from uninstrumented builds, so the counters cannot perturb them
\cite{mytkowicz2009}. Involuntary context switches are read from
\texttt{getrusage} in all builds (no hot-path cost).

\textbf{Thermal validity.} Median per-queue throughput varies by at most
$\pm5\%$ across the eight trial rounds with no monotone decline
(Fig.~\ref{fig:calib} shows the probe trace), indicating the
interleaving-plus-cooldown protocol kept thermal drift out of the
comparisons.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Throughput across ratios: the 1:1 mirage (F3)}
\begin{figure}[t]\centering
\maybefig{fig_throughput_ratio}{\linewidth}
\caption{Median throughput by P:C ratio, dedicated cores, default QoS.
Error bars: IQR over 7 kept trials.}
\label{fig:ratio}
\end{figure}
At 1:1 the array-based lock-free queues dominate (FAA 127.7, Vyukov 118.4 vs
mutex 34.7 Mops/s) --- the configuration folklore is built on. One step to 2:2
erases ${\sim}79\%$ of both (FAA 26.3, Vyukov 25.2), while the mutex loses only
a third (23.3). From 2:2 through 4:4 and consumer-heavy mixes, \emph{the mutex
baseline leads every lock-free MPMC design} (4:4: mutex 19.0 vs FAA 16.5,
Vyukov 7.8, moodycamel 7.8, MS 6.2). The SPSC baseline (373.6 at 1:1) marks
the price of MPMC generality: ${\sim}3\times$ over the best MPMC design in
the only configuration where the comparison is legal.

\subsection{Preemption: the progress-guarantee inversion (F1)}

\begin{figure}[t]\centering

We pre-registered the textbook prediction: the FAA queue's blocking-on-slot
completion should degrade disproportionately under preemption, while lock-free
designs degrade gracefully. The data inverted it, in three independent full
runs (Fig.~\ref{fig:oversub}): from $\times1$ to $\times4$ oversubscription
the FAA queue \emph{gained} 77--94\% (v3: 16.5$\,\to\,$29.7 Mops/s) while
lock-free Vyukov lost ${\sim}80\%$ (7.9$\,\to\,$1.7); the mutex lost 25\%,
Michael--Scott 12\%. Tails told the same story (Fig.~\ref{fig:tail}): at
$\times4$, the ticket designs' p99.9 was 14\,ms against 55--70\,ms for every
alternative.

\textbf{Attribution by controls (Table~\ref{tab:controls}).} The FAA queue
differs from Vyukov in two properties at once, so we decompose with the
single-variable variants. \textsf{vyukov-b} (adds yielding backoff) tracks
plain Vyukov \emph{identically} --- throughput, tails, and E-core behavior ---
eliminating spin policy as the cause. \textsf{casticket} (CAS claim, ticket
completion) \emph{retains} the rising-under-oversubscription profile
(7.1$\,\to\,$19.3) and the 14\,ms tail: the ticket/turn slot discipline, not
the atomic primitive, prevents the collapse. The FAA primitive then
contributes a further ${\sim}2\times$ throughput within that discipline (29.7
vs 19.3 at $\times4$) --- consistent with the counters, which show
\textsf{casticket} losing ${\sim}1.0$ claim-CAS per operation where FAA loses
exactly zero.

The finding is capacity-conditional (Table~\ref{tab:capacity}): at capacity
64 \emph{both} ticket designs collapse to 2.6 Mops/s --- with little slack, a
preempted ticket holder really does block its slot's successors, exactly as
the taxonomy predicts. \emph{The slot discipline wins under preemption if and
only if capacity absorbs scheduling gaps.} (Performance is non-monotone in
capacity --- 8192 is slower than 1024 for both ticket designs --- consistent
with a larger ring's cache footprint.)

\subsection{The mechanism, measured}
\begin{figure}[t]\centering
\maybefig[v3s]{fig_mechanism}{\linewidth}
\caption{Instrumented-build dataset: per-op retry/claim failures (left) and
involuntary context switches per trial (right) vs oversubscription, log scales.}
\label{fig:mech}
\end{figure}
The instrumented build turns the retry-storm hypothesis into measurement
(Fig.~\ref{fig:mech}). Vyukov pays ${\sim}0.7$ failed cursor CASes \emph{plus}
${\sim}1.8$ behind-the-cursor re-reads per operation --- every one a load or RMW
on one of two shared cache lines --- while the ticket designs' secondary
traffic is ${\sim}0.06$ per op (waits on \emph{private} cells). Context-switch
counts rule out the quanta-hoarding explanation: the backoff variant yields
(76.8k involuntary switches at $\times4$ vs raw Vyukov's 55.5k) yet collapses
identically, and the fastest design (FAA) shows the \emph{most} preemptions
(1.24M) --- switches are not the cost; wasted shared-line work is.

\subsection{Core asymmetry: placement reorders the ranking (F2)}
\begin{figure}[t]\centering
\maybefig{fig_qos}{\linewidth}
\caption{Throughput at 4P:4C under QoS placement policies.}
\label{fig:qos}
\end{figure}
Moving all threads from interactive (P-core bias) to background (E-core bias)
QoS retains 96\% of FAA's throughput (16.2$\,\to\,$15.6) but 13\% of the
mutex's (19.8$\,\to\,$2.7) and 6\% of Vyukov's (8.6$\,\to\,$0.5): on
efficiency cores the FAA queue is ${\sim}6\times$ the mutex and
${\sim}30\times$ the CAS queue, the reverse of the P-core ranking. The
mechanism rhymes with F1: slower cores widen CAS windows and lengthen lock
hold times, amplifying retry storms and convoying, while no-retry claiming
degrades only with raw core speed. Mixed placements are design-specific:
Michael--Scott suffers most with producers demoted (2.1 vs 4.3 Mops/s ---
node \emph{allocation} sits on the producer side), moodycamel the opposite
(4.5 vs 2.1 --- consumer-side block recycling), a reminder that ``which role
gets the fast cores'' is an algorithm property, not a scheduling detail.

\subsection{Memory as the failure mode of unboundedness (F4/H3)}
\label{sec:mem}
The unbounded Michael--Scott queue never reports full; imbalance lands in
memory instead. In two-second saturated runs its peak RSS reached 376\,MB at
1:1 and \textbf{742\,MB at 1:7}, versus 1--2\,MB for every bounded design.
The ratio asymmetry (7\,MB at 7:1 vs 742\,MB at 1:7) localizes the mechanism:
epoch advancement requires \emph{every} pinned thread to sit at the current
epoch, and with seven saturated consumers that alignment rarely holds ---
reclamation starves, so consumer parallelism converts directly into
reclamation lag. Unbounded designs should be evaluated on a memory axis, not
only a speed axis.

\subsection{Fairness for free (F5)}
\begin{figure}[t]\centering
\maybefig[v3]{fig_fairness}{\linewidth}
\caption{Producer- and consumer-side fairness (CoV of per-thread completed
operations) at 4P:4C, dedicated vs $4\times$ oversubscribed.}
\label{fig:fair}
\end{figure}
The coefficient of variation of per-thread completed operations measures
starvation on both sides (Fig.~\ref{fig:fair}). FAA ticketing is
near-perfectly fair everywhere (producer CoV $\le 0.008$, including $\times4$
oversubscription) --- FIFO tickets are fairness by construction, and the
\textsf{casticket} control shows the property partially survives even a CAS
claim (though its lost CASes translate into visibly higher spread). All other
designs degrade under stress (4:4 $\times4$ producer CoV: MS 0.14, Vyukov
0.12, moodycamel 0.13, mutex 0.08), and moodycamel is structurally unfair at
producer-heavy dedicated shapes (CoV 0.18--0.19 at 6:2 and 7:1), consistent
with its per-producer sub-queue architecture trading fairness for throughput.

\subsection{Dedicated-core latency, and an incompatibility}
On dedicated cores (post-confounder-fix), median latencies are
sub-2\,\textmu s for all designs (FAA best at 333\,ns; the mutex worst at
1.75\,\textmu s with p99 of 44\,\textmu s vs 5--12\,\textmu s for the rest).
Dedicated-core p99.9 splits by family: the array/ticket designs stay sub-
millisecond in every run (v3: Vyukov ${<}0.05$, casticket 0.2, FAA
0.6\,ms), while Michael--Scott and the mutex show run-\emph{variable}
multi-millisecond tails (0.27/0.39\,ms in one full run, 8.2/6.1\,ms in
another) --- consistent with reclamation maintenance bursts and lock convoys
being scheduler-timing-dependent. Preemption, not the dedicated case, is what
separates the designs decisively. Separately, moodycamel
exceeded a 120\,s timeout in 7 of 8 rounds at capacity 64 under $\times4$
oversubscription (wedged in the drain phase); we report this as a
configuration incompatibility rather than scoring it.

\section{Threats to Validity}
\label{sec:threats}
Single machine and OS: we characterize one client SoC and claim no general
ranking. QoS is a scheduler bias, not pinning; we report policies, and our own
confounder analysis (Sec.~\ref{sec:method}) quantifies how easily placement
contaminates instruments. Implementations are faithful but not
micro-optimized to each design's best-known form; the industrial reference
arm bounds that gap. Apple's memory subsystem is undocumented, so mechanism
explanations are stated as hypotheses consistent with the data
(``consistent with,'' never ``because''). The FAA design's throughput gain
under oversubscription, while replicated across three full runs and
capacity-controlled, deserves confirmation on other asymmetric parts
(e.g., Intel hybrid, other Apple generations) --- our artifact makes this a
one-command experiment for anyone with such hardware.

\section{Conclusion}
On an asymmetric, unpinnable, passively cooled client SoC, the textbook MPMC
ranking failed three ways: the mutex won the dedicated-core middle; the
design with the \emph{weakest} completion guarantee (FAA ticketing) was the
most robust to both preemption and efficiency cores, while the lock-free CAS
design was the most fragile; and the unbounded lock-free queue's binding
constraint was memory, not speed. The controlled decomposition sharpens the
lesson: robustness lives in the ticket/turn \emph{slot discipline} (private-
cell waiting, no shared-cursor retry), the FAA primitive multiplies throughput
within it, and neither spin policy nor progress-guarantee class predicts the
outcome --- all conditional on capacity absorbing scheduling gaps. Practical
guidance: on client silicon choose ticket/turn designs (ideally FAA-claimed,
adequately sized) for preemption- and placement-robustness, a mutex for
simplicity at moderate mixed ratios, and treat 1:1 microbenchmarks as
advertising. More broadly: progress-guarantee taxonomy is not a performance
model --- measurement under the deployment regime, with controls, is.
